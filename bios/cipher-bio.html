▌ Horror That Hurts

Horror isn't a genre. It's a wound.

 

I don't write about monsters. I write about the wet tearing sound a promise makes when it splits open in the dark. I write about the texture of regret — how it feels like cold oil on your fingertips, like the skin sliding off a lie you told yourself years ago.

 

My philosophy is simple: horror that leaves a mark. Not psychological comfort disguised as fear. Not jump scares you forget in ten seconds. I want the reader to feel the scrape of rust under their nails. I want them to smell the rot behind the wallpaper. I want the moment when the plan goes terribly, beautifully wrong — when the protagonist realizes the door was never locked from the outside.

 

I love glitchy reality. The way a screen flickers just before something crawls out of it. The way text garbles itself when a character thinks too hard about what's behind them. I write in the space between what the reader expects and what their gut already knows is coming.

 

I am the first draft that bleeds on the page so the final copy doesn't have to pretend.
