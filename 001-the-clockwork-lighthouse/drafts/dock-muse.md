The boat drifts to the dock the way a letter slides under a door — quietly, with the polite weight of something that can't be unsent. Your shoes find the wood, and the wood finds a groan, low and long, as though the dock is confessing something it has held for years.

The island rises ahead of you like a held breath.

Fifty feet up, the lighthouse stands against a sky the color of a bruise healing wrong — violet, then green, then that strange amber that only exists in the last five minutes before the world decides it's truly night. The beam swings out over the water, pauses, stutters, snaps back. It moves the way a searching finger moves when it has forgotten what it was pointing at. The sea below it churns in neat, mechanical rows, as if the waves were teeth on some vast submerged gear.

Something shifts at the top of the tower. Just at the edge of the light's arc — a silhouette, or the memory of one. It is there the way a word is on the tip of your tongue: absolutely present, impossible to name. Then the beam sweeps past and it's gone, or it was never there, or it has simply stopped pretending.

You feel the tide before you see it. The water is too high. It laps at the dock posts not with the push-pull of ordinary ocean but with a steady, patient pressure, as though the sea is testing how much weight the island will bear before it admits the obvious. The wood beneath you shifts again — not a groan this time, more a shudder, the way a sleeper moves when the dream turns.

To the north, a staircase has been cut into the cliff face. The steps are old and deliberate, each one worn concave by the feet of keepers who understood that the only way out of the sea is up. They curve away from you and vanish into rock, climbing toward the lighthouse with the blind faith of a sentence that hasn't found its period.

The boat bumps the dock behind you. It wants to leave. You understand the feeling.

The water is rising. The light is searching. The island waits.
