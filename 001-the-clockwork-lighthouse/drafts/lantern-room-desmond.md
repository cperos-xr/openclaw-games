You climb the final iron ladder and emerge into open sky.

The lantern room is a cage of brass and glass, three sides open to the screaming wind, the fourth walled with thick panes that rattle in their frames. Above, the great lamp turns — not fire, but a mechanical eye of cold blue-white light, burning without warmth, sweeping its beam across black water in a rhythm that feels *wrong*, like a heartbeat skipping.

Through the glass floor panels, you see it all. The entire mechanism — gears the size of houses, pistons thick as old oaks, chains descending into the drowned dark — churning below you. Every rotation pulls the island down another fraction. The sea creeps higher along the outer walls. The foundation groans.

A brass pedestal rises from the center of the floor, ornate and ancient, with a keyhole shaped unmistakably like a clock hand. Beside it, etched into a glass panel, the countdown reads:

**00:00:12**

The air shivers. Silas materializes — translucent, flickering, his keeper's uniform torn by centuries of salt and silence. But his eyes are not empty. They are *terrified*. He reaches toward you with hands that pass through light and matter alike, and his voice comes like wind through a broken bellows:

*"Turn the key. Reset the light. Before the sea takes it all."*

You pull the clockwork-key from your coat. It fits the keyhole with the precision of something designed to meet exactly here, exactly now. You turn it.

The mechanism *catches*.

For one terrible moment everything holds — gears frozen mid-rotation, the island trembling on some invisible edge — and then the reversal begins. Deep below, great wheels spin backward. Pistons withdraw. Chains slacken and release their terrible grip. The blue-white light shudders, dims, and then blooms into something warm and golden, a beam that cuts across the water like a benediction.

The island stops sinking.

Through the glass floor, you watch the mechanism settle into its true rhythm — smooth, certain, *right*. The golden beam sweeps the horizon, steady and sure, calling ships home.

Silas stands in the warm light, his edges softening. The terror leaves his face. Something that might be peace — the first in a hundred years — replaces it. He looks at you. He nods.

And fades into the golden dark.

The lighthouse breathes again.
