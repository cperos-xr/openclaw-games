The motorboat scraped against stone and died. You tied the rope to the nearest post — salt-rotted wood that crumbled under your fingers — and stepped onto the island.

The dock stretched before you like a broken spine, planks warped and black with brine. Every third piling had split; the ones that remained stood at uneven angles, as though the sea were slowly pulling them under. And it was. The tide lapped at the dock's edge not with the push-pull of ordinary ocean but with a steady, patient pressure — a hand inching across a table toward something it meant to take.

Above you, fifty feet of wet cliff rose to where the lighthouse stood. It was old — iron-streaked, barnacled, its white paint long since surrendered to the weather — and it leaned, just slightly, toward the sea. As if it had been watching the water for so long it had forgotten how to stand upright. The sky behind it was the color of a bruise healing wrong: violet, then green, then that strange amber that only exists in the last five minutes before the world decides it's truly night.

The beam swept.

A lance of cold white light tore across the churning waves, paused, shuddered, then lurched onward in a stuttering arc. You could hear the mechanism even from here — a grinding at irregular intervals, like a clock with a broken tooth on its gear. Each sweep cast the waves in stark relief for a half-second: whitecaps frozen mid-curl, black water turning itself inside out, like teeth on some vast submerged machine.

Something moved.

At the top of the tower, in the instant the beam swept past — a shape. Human, or close to it. Standing where no one should be, just beyond the lantern room glass. It was there the way a word is on the tip of your tongue: absolutely present, impossible to name. Then the light passed and it was gone, or it was never there, or it had simply stopped pretending.

The dock groaned beneath you — a deep, wet sound, the whole structure shifting as another surge of tide pushed inland. Not the sound of weight. The sound of something pulling away. Water now covered the first step of the stone staircase carved into the cliff face.

The stairs switchbacked up the rock, narrow and glistening, with no railing and the sea waiting twenty feet below. Each step had been worn concave by the feet of keepers who understood that the only way out of the sea is up. They curved away from you and vanished into the cliff, climbing toward the lighthouse door with the blind faith of a sentence that hasn't found its period.

Behind you, the boat bumped the dock. It wanted to leave. You understood the feeling.

The island was sinking. The only way was up.
