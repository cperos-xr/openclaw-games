You climb the spiral stair and the air changes — grows thick, personal, like entering someone's mouth. The keeper's quarters open before you the way a wound opens: small, precise, and wrong.

The room has been living without you. A threadbare blanket on the cot still holds the ghost of a body's depression, as though Silas Thorne merely stepped out for air and forgot to return for fifty years. His mug sits on the nightstand. Something dark and dry coats the bottom. The maritime charts pinned to the walls have yellowed into parchment-thin skins, their coastlines wrinkling like the palms of old sailors reading futures that never arrived.

Then there is the portrait.

Silas Thorne watches you from a frame of salt-blackened oak. His face is a clenched fist — every bone fighting to escape the skin, eyes sunk so deep they've found something beneath bone. You move left. The eyes follow. You move right. They follow. Not the trick of a painted gaze. Something more deliberate. His mouth is closed, but you get the sense he is counting.

A heavy trunk squats at the foot of the cot like a toad that swallowed something valuable. The lock is rusted shut, patient, waiting for the right key — or the right fool.

You lift the pillow and find a single page torn from a journal. The handwriting slants violently, as though the words were running from the pen. Five words survive the deterioration:

*The mechanism must not reach zero.*

You set the page down gently, the way you'd set down a live thing. Somewhere below you — beneath the floor, beneath the rock, beneath everything — something ticks. Not a clock. Clocks forgive. This sound has teeth.

The portrait's eyes have not blinked.
