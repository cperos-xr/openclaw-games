The door groans open onto a room that smells of salt and rust and old grease. You have to turn sideways to fit between the shelving units — they press in from both sides like the ribs of some long-dead thing. The ceiling is low enough to touch. Every surface is cluttered: coils of rope gone stiff and brown, tins with their labels dissolved to grey pulp, wrenches and spanners heaped in corroded piles. Someone organized this once. You can see the ghost of a system — hooks labeled with faded ink, shelves divided by type of tool. But that was before whoever worked here stopped caring.

Your boots squelch. The floor is wet. Not puddles — something worse. A slow, steady seep of seawater pushing up through hairline cracks in the concrete, darkening the stone in spreading stains. You feel the dampness climb your ankles and think: the island is sinking. It has been sinking, probably for years. This room is below the waterline now, and the sea wants it back.

The workbench takes up most of the back wall. Brass components are scattered across its surface — gears, pins, tiny threaded cylinders — as though someone was mid-repair and simply walked away. You sweep aside the parts and find the symbol. Scratched deep into the wood with a blade or a nail: a circle with a vertical line through it, bisecting a smaller triangle. Not recent. The edges have gone soft with swelling grain. You don't know what it means. You trace it with your fingertip and feel the gouges catch on your skin.

On the bench's far edge, half-buried under a canvas rag, sits a heavy tin of machine oil. The label is gone, but when you shake it, something thick and viscous sloshes inside. Still usable. You take it.

By the door — on a hook you almost missed — hangs a small iron key. Rust has eaten into its surface like a disease, but the teeth are intact. Whatever it opens, it was left here deliberately. Waiting.

You pocket the key and squeeze back out. The room will be underwater soon enough.
