The boat bumps against the dock with a hollow sound, and you tie off the line to the nearest post. It's soft under your fingers — the wood is rotting, black with salt and age. You step onto the island.

The lighthouse rises above you, maybe fifty feet, its stone column dark against a sky the color of a bruise. The beam at its crown rotates in a slow, broken rhythm — sweep, stutter, sweep, two seconds of darkness, then back. The light catches the waves below in strobing white flashes. The water is wrong. It's too high. It laps at the base of the dock where it shouldn't reach, curling over the rocks in dark, oily tongues. You can hear it breathing against the stone.

The dock groans beneath you. Not the sound of weight — the sound of something pulling away. The posts are leaning. The whole structure tilts imperceptibly toward the sea, as if the island is slowly letting go of it.

You look up.

Something moves.

At the top of the lighthouse, where the beam begins its arc, there is a shape. Just for a moment — a silhouette caught in the sweep of light. It's too large to be a bird, too still to be a curtain. Then the beam stutters past and the window goes dark. You watch for another rotation. Nothing. Just the glass, catching light and throwing it out to sea.

The wind carries a sound you can't place. Not mechanical. Not quite organic. Something between a hum and a groan, low enough to feel in your chest rather than hear with your ears.

Behind you, the boat rocks. The rope holds. For now.

Ahead, a staircase is cut into the cliff face — old stone steps, slick with spray, winding upward toward the lighthouse door. They're narrow. They don't have a railing. The waves crash twenty feet below them, and with the tide this high, the lowest steps are already underwater.

The island is sinking, and the only way is up.
