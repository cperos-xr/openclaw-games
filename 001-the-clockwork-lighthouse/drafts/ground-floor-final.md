# Ground Floor — The Clockwork Lighthouse

The room breathes.

Not with air — with motion. The circular stone chamber pulses at a frequency you feel in your teeth, a deep mechanical hum rising from somewhere beneath the floor. Gears — thousands of them — mesh in darkness behind the masonry, a clockwork skeleton hidden in the walls, and the building shudders with their turning. The air tastes of rust and machine oil. Something vast and toothed works ceaselessly behind the plaster, and the sound is not quite sound. It is pressure. A grinding, insistent reminder that the lighthouse has always been *working*, and that you have interrupted nothing.

A wooden desk commands the center of the room, scarred and water-stained. Papers are scattered across it in disarray — some pinned under a stone paperweight, others drifting toward the floor as the building's vibration nudges them toward escape. The handwriting is frantic: looped and jagged by turns, ink blotting where the pen dug too deep. The same words recur across multiple pages with the fervor of a man writing to stay sane — *the countdown*, *when the gears reach zero*, and a name pressed so hard it nearly tore the paper: *Silas*. One sheet bears only a number, written over and over, growing larger with each repetition.

Beneath the papers sits a leather-bound journal, its cover embossed with gear diagrams so intricate they seem to shift in the dim light. Inside, hand-drawn schematics spill across the pages — interlocking mechanisms annotated in a cramped, frantic hand. The margins overflow with notes that trail into nothing, as though the writer kept losing his train of thought, or kept being *interrupted*.

A rusted iron door stands sealed to the east, its surface pocked with corrosion. A small keyhole gapes near the handle like a wound. You try the latch. Locked tight. A narrow door west leads toward what must be a supply room, darkness pooling beyond it like ink. A spiral staircase of black iron corkscrews upward along the far wall, its steps worn smooth at the center from decades of footsteps, rising into shadow.

The hum drops half a tone. The gears shift behind the walls, and for a moment — just a moment — the vibration matches the rhythm of a heartbeat.
