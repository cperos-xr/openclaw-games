# Keeper's Quarters — Final Scene

The spiral staircase delivers you into a room that breathes — not with air, but with absence.

Fifty years of salt and silence should have reduced this place to driftwood. They haven't. The cot's blanket lies folded back as though someone rose from it moments ago. A tin mug sits on the desk, ringed with the ghost of coffee. The pillow holds a faint depression — as though the sleeper merely stepped out and will return before the dust settles.

He won't.

Maritime charts paper the walls, their edges curling like dying leaves, each annotated in a cramped, frantic hand. Lighthouse positions circled in red ink. Margins filled with symbols you don't recognize — steadier in the early pages, then devolving into desperation, the coastlines redrawn over and over as if the drawer feared forgetting their shape. A pencil snaps in two on the desk. Both pieces point the same direction: toward the window, toward the sea.

And above the cot, the portrait.

Silas Thorne regards you from a water-stained frame. Gaunt. Hollowed cheeks, eyes sunk so deep they seem to exist in a different room altogether. The oil has cracked along his cheekbones like fault lines in dry earth, but his eyes are untouched by time. They follow the exact center of your chest — not your face — as though watching something behind your ribs. You step left. The gaze tracks. You step right. It tracks. There is something wrong with his mouth. Not a smile. Not a grimace. The expression of a man who learned a secret and cannot decide whether to laugh or scream.

At the foot of the cot sits a heavy iron-banded trunk. You test the lid. Locked. The rusted keyhole gapes at you like a wound.

Beneath the pillow — a single page torn from a journal. The handwriting deteriorates mid-sentence, from careful script to jagged slashes:

*The mechanism must not reach zero. I have said it before and I will say it until the ink runs dry or the sea takes my hands — the mechanism must not reach zero. God forgive me, I cannot remember what happens when it does.*

The page trembles in your grip. Or perhaps it is your hands.

Something behind the portrait's eyes shifts — a trick of light, surely — and you become aware that you are alone in a way that does not feel like solitude.
