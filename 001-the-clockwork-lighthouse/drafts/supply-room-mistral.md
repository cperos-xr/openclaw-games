# Supply Room — Draft by The Muse (writer-mistral-creative)

The door stuck, then gave with a screech that echoed in the cramped space. You squeezed through and nearly knocked a tin can off a shelf with your elbow. The room was barely wider than your armspan, choked with the detritus of maintenance: rusted tools hung from pegboard like bones in an ossuary, coiled rope sat in stiff loops on the floor, and tin cans — hundreds of them — lined the shelves in rows that had partially collapsed into a landslide of labels and corrosion.

The air tasted of rust and something older. Seawater.

You looked down. Dark stains spread across the floorboards, and between two planks a thin rivulet of water caught the light. The cracks were widening. The sea was finding its way in, patient and inevitable, the way it finds its way into everything.

The workbench dominated the far wall, a slab of driftwood scarred by decades of use. Brass components lay scattered across it — gears no bigger than coins, springs that had lost all tension, tiny cogs that might have belonged to a watch or to something much larger. Someone had been tinkering here, right up until the end.

Then you noticed the symbol. Scratched deep into the workbench surface with a blade or a nail, it was a circle with teeth — like a gear, but the teeth were wrong. Uneven. Organic. The kind of shape that made your brain itch.

A tin of machine oil sat on the lower shelf, surprisingly heavy. The label had long since rusted away, but when you shook it, something sloshed inside. Still usable.

And there, on a hook by the door where you almost missed it — a small iron key, dark with age. Someone had hung it there on purpose, within reach of the exit. Ready for a quick grab.

You pocketed both. The floor groaned under your weight, and the water in the cracks seemed to rise another fraction of an inch.
