# Ground Floor — Draft by Desmond Vex (writer-deepseek)

You pushed through the door and the sound hit you first — a low, pervasive hum that you felt in your molars before you heard it. The walls were *vibrating*. Not shaking, not trembling — vibrating with the deep, rhythmic pulse of machinery running somewhere behind the plaster. Gears. Dozens of them, maybe hundreds, turning in the dark behind the curved stone walls. You could trace the vibrations by touch: a groove here where something large turned, a patch of warmth there where friction had worn through.

The room was circular, maybe twenty feet across. A spiral staircase in the center wound upward into darkness, its iron banister slick with condensation. The steps were worn smooth in the center — someone had climbed them thousands of times.

Against the far wall, a wooden desk sat beneath a boarded window. Papers were scattered across it — not fallen, but *thrown*, as if someone had swept everything off and then carefully placed some of it back. You picked through the pages. Tide calculations. Gear ratios. And then, scrawled in increasingly frantic handwriting: *"The countdown cannot be stopped. Only reset."* The name "Silas" appeared over and over, sometimes underlined, sometimes circled, once scratched through so hard the pen tore the paper.

A leather-bound journal sat half-hidden under the desk's edge. You opened it and found hand-drawn diagrams of gears and clockwork — technical, precise, the work of an engineer. But as the pages progressed, the drawings grew wild. Spirals. Impossible geometries. Notes in the margins that made your eyes slide off the words as if they refused to be read.

The locked door was east — iron, rusted solid, with a keyhole no bigger than a pencil. Something behind it. You pressed your ear to the cold metal and heard... not silence. The grinding of more gears, deeper and louder than the walls.

A narrow door to the west stood ajar, leading into shadow. The supply room, maybe.

The ceiling above was dark with old smoke stains, spiraling outward from the lamp fixture like a fingerprint. Or like the shadow of a very large gear.

The hum never stopped.
