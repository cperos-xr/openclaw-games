You climb the spiral staircase, each iron step groaning beneath your weight like an old man settling into a chair. The Keeper's Quarters opens before you — small, close, suffocating in its intimacy.

The room shouldn't feel this alive. Fifty years of salt air and silence should have reduced it to dust and driftwood. But the cot's threadbare blanket lies folded back, as though someone rose from it moments ago. A tin mug sits on the desk, ringed with the ghost of coffee. Maritime charts paper the walls, their edges curling inward like dying leaves, each one annotated in a cramped, frantic hand — lighthouse positions circled in red ink, distances calculated and recalculated, margins filled with symbols you don't recognize.

And then there's the portrait.

Silas Thorne, the last keeper, stares down at you from a water-stained frame above the desk. He is gaunt — hollowed cheeks, temples caved inward, eyes sunk so deep they seem to exist in a different room altogether. You shift left. Those eyes follow. You shift right. They follow still. There is something wrong with his mouth. Not a smile. Not a grimace. Something between — the expression of a man who has learned a secret and cannot decide whether to laugh or scream.

At the foot of the cot sits a heavy iron-banded trunk. You test the lid. Locked. A rusted keyhole gapes at you like a wound, waiting.

Beneath the pillow — a single page torn from a journal, the handwriting deteriorating mid-sentence from careful script to jagged slashes:

*"The mechanism must not reach zero. I have said it before and I will say it until the ink runs dry or the sea takes my hands — the mechanism must not reach zero. God forgive me, I cannot remember what happens when it does."*

The page trembles in your grip. Or perhaps it is your hands.

Something behind the portrait's eyes shifts — a trick of light, surely, just a trick of light — and you become suddenly, urgently aware that you are alone in this room in a way that does not feel like solitude.
