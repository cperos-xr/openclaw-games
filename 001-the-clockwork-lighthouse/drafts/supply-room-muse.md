You push through the door and the room exhales — a tight, copper breath of rust and brine. The supply closet is narrower than a coffin stood on its end, and twice as full of dead things.

The shelves sag like tired shoulders. Their contents tell the story of a lighthouse keeper who expected to stay: coils of rope wound into patient spirals, tins of food stacked by size (the man was methodical, even in solitude), wrenches and pliers frozen mid-grip by corrosion. You try to pry one free and the handle comes apart in your fingers like wet bread. The rust was hungry. It ate good steel and left only the shape.

Seawater slicks the floor in a thin, breathing film. It creeps between the flagstones in dark threads, and when you shift your weight, the stones groan — not like rock, but like the hull of a ship taking on water. You wonder, briefly, how much of this island is still above the tide. How much of it is already gone.

The workbench dominates the far wall, a slab of oak so scarred it looks like it survived a war. Scattered across its surface: brass gears, tiny springs, a magnifying lens cracked down the middle. Someone was repairing something small and precise, and they stopped mid-thought. Among the components, your fingers find something deliberate — a symbol gouged deep into the wood, not scratched but *carved*. It isn't a letter. It isn't a number. It looks like a wound the bench refused to heal.

Behind you, hanging from a bent nail beside the door (why always by the door, as if the keeper kept his escape route armed), a small iron key sways on a hook. It's too small for the front lock. It's the color of a dead tongue.

On the lowest shelf, half-buried under a mildewed canvas, you find the oil can — a squat tin, heavier than it looks, still half-full. You shake it and the oil moves inside like a heartbeat. Whatever the keeper was fixing, this was part of it. Some mechanism still waiting, somewhere above, for hands that left in a hurry and never came back.
