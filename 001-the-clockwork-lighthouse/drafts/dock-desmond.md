The motorboat scraped against stone and died. You tied the rope to the nearest post — salt-rotted wood that crumbled under your fingers — and stepped onto the island.

The dock stretched before you like a broken spine, planks warped and black with brine. Every third piling had split; the ones that remained stood at uneven angles, as though the sea were slowly pulling them under. And it was. The tide lapped at the dock's edge with a patience that felt deliberate, a hand inching across a table toward something it meant to take. Already the lowest stones of the path were slick with water.

Above you, fifty feet of wet cliff rose to where the lighthouse stood. It was old — iron-streaked, barnacled, its white paint long since surrendered to the weather — and it leaned, just slightly, toward the sea. As if it had been watching the water for so long it had forgotten how to stand upright.

The beam swept.

A lance of cold white light tore across the churning waves, paused, shuddered, then lurched onward in a stuttering arc. The mechanism was wrong — you could hear it even from here, a grinding at irregular intervals, like a clock with a broken tooth on its gear. The light made a full rotation, then seized, then jumped. Each sweep cast the waves in stark relief for a half-second: whitecaps frozen mid-curl, black water turning itself inside out.

Something moved.

At the top of the tower, in the instant the beam swept past — a shape. Human, or close to it. Standing where no one should be, just beyond the lantern room glass. The light passed and it was gone. You told yourself it was a trick of motion, the afterimage of the beam burned into your retinas.

The dock groaned beneath you. A deep, wet sound, the whole structure shifting as another surge of tide pushed inland. Water now covered the first step of the stone staircase carved into the cliff face. The stairs switchbacked up the rock, narrow and glistening, with no railing and the sea waiting below.

You could still reach the boat. The engine might start, if you were lucky. If you left now.

The lighthouse beam swept back across the waves, and in its passing you saw the shape again — still there, still watching.

The stairs were the only way up.
