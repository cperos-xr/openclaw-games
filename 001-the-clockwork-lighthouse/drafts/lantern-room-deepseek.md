# Lantern Room — Draft by Desmond Vex (writer-deepseek)

The stairs ended. The world opened.

The lantern room was the crown of the lighthouse — open to the sky on three sides, the fourth walled with thick glass panels that warped the sea into a rippling, vertical ocean. Wind screamed through the gaps, carrying salt spray that froze on your skin. Above, the sky was black. Not night-black — *void*-black. No stars. No moon. Just darkness that pressed down like a weight.

And in the center, the lamp.

It wasn't fire. It wasn't electricity. It was something else — a mechanical heart of glass and brass that burned with a cold, blue-white radiance that cast no warmth and threw shadows in directions that didn't match any light source. The beam emerged from its crystal core and swept outward through the glass wall, cutting across the churning sea in a blade of wrong light. It stuttered. It pulsed. It was dying.

Through the glass floor panels — thick, warped, like standing on frozen water — you could see the entire mechanism below. Gears turning. Chains tightening. The whole lighthouse was visible from here, a cross-section of clockwork heart, and you could see the island sinking. The water was at the base of the cliffs now. The dock was gone. The waves were climbing.

The countdown read 00:00:12.

Twelve seconds.

A brass pedestal stood in the center of the room, directly beneath the lamp. It was simple — a column of bronze with a single keyhole shaped like a clock hand. The clockwork-key in your pocket grew warm. You reached for it.

The air shimmered.

He appeared between you and the pedestal — not suddenly, but the way a word appears when you finally remember it. A presence that had always been there, now visible. Gaunt. Hollow-cheeked. Eyes like polished stones.

Silas Thorne. The last keeper. Dead for fifty years.

But not gone.

His mouth moved. The wind carried his words to you like they'd been waiting: *"Turn the key. Reset the light. Before the sea takes it all."*

He stepped aside. Not because he was afraid. Because he was *trusting*. He'd held this place together for half a century, alone, dead, refusing to let the countdown reach zero. And now someone had come.

You stepped forward. The pedestal accepted the clockwork-key with a click that echoed through the mechanism below, through every gear and chain and shaft, through the stone walls and the iron bones of the lighthouse. You turned it.

The mechanism *reversed*.

Below you, gears spun backward, then forward, then found their rhythm. The grinding stopped. The stuttering stopped. The hum shifted — from laborious death-rattle to the deep, steady pulse of something *alive*. The countdown ticked up: 00:01:00. 00:05:00. 00:10:00. Reset. Stabilized. *Running.*

The lamp changed. The blue-white wrongness faded, and in its place — warmth. Golden light, the color of sunset through honey, poured from the crystal core and swept across the sea in a steady, reliable beam. No stutter. No flicker. A lighthouse. Doing what lighthouses do.

Below, through the glass floor, you watched the water retreat. The island rose. The cliffs emerged, dripping, and the sea fell back to its proper line. The dock reappeared — battered, groaning, but above water. Your motorboat still bobbed at its post.

Silas stood at the glass wall, looking out at the golden beam cutting across the waves. The wind touched him and he didn't ripple. He was solid. Real. *Here.*

He turned to you. His expression wasn't urgent anymore. It was the face from the portrait — but at peace. The look of a man who had finally finished his watch.

He didn't speak. He didn't need to. He raised one hand — the gesture from the lens room, the shadow in the refractions — and then he was light. Golden light, the same color as the beam, folding into the lamp's core and becoming part of its steady, warm, unending pulse.

The lighthouse keeper had gone home.

The beam swept the sea. The island held. The gears turned. Somewhere below, the mechanism counted up from zero, measuring not time remaining, but time *preserved*.

You stood in the lantern room and breathed.
