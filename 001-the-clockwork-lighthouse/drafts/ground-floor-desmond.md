The room breathes.

Not with air — with motion. The circular stone walls vibrate at a frequency you feel in your teeth, a deep mechanical hum that rises from somewhere beneath the floor and resonates through the plaster like a living pulse. Something vast and toothed turns behind these walls, gears meshing in the dark, and the sound is not quite sound. It is pressure. A grinding, ceaseless insistence that the building is *working*, that it has always been working, and that you have interrupted nothing.

A narrow door leads west — old wood, warped in its frame, opening toward what must be a supply room. East, a rusted iron door stands sealed, its surface pocked with corrosion like something has been trying to eat through it from the other side. A small keyhole stares at you from its center, dark as a wound. The door does not rattle when you test it. It does not need to. It is patient.

A spiral staircase coils upward along the far wall, its iron steps bolted into stone, rising into shadow. The gears seem louder near it, as though the mechanism climbs the tower with you, riding the walls like ivy made of teeth.

The desk is what draws you. Old wood, scarred and water-stained, pushed against the plaster as if someone hid behind it once. Papers litter its surface — most are illegible, bleached by damp and time, but two words recur across them with the fervor of a man writing to stay sane: *the countdown*. And a name, scrawled harder each time, pressed so deep into the paper it nearly tore: *Silas*.

Beneath the papers, a leather-bound journal. Its cover is stamped with a gear emblem, and when you open it, hand-drawn diagrams spill out — schematics of interlocking mechanisms, annotated in a cramped, frantic hand. The margins overflow with notes that trail into nothing, as though the writer kept losing his train of thought or kept being *interrupted*.

The hum drops half a tone. The gears shift behind the walls, and for a moment — just a moment — the vibration matches the rhythm of a heartbeat.
