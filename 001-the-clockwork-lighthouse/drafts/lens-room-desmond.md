The Lens Room is a cathedral of glass.

The great Fresnel lens rises before you like a crystal beehive — concentric rings of prismatic glass mounted in a brass armature that groans as it rotates. Light enters from somewhere above, passes through the apparatus, and emerges fractured into a hundred beams that sweep the narrow walkway in slow, jerky arcs. It is beautiful. It is the most beautiful thing you have ever seen, and it is wrong.

The light scatters in patterns too deliberate for refraction. You see shapes in the beams — geometries that almost resolve into faces, into reaching hands, into mouths open in silent instruction. They hang in the air for a moment, burning afterimages into your retinas, then dissolve as the lens grinds another quarter-turn.

The shadows are worse.

They move *in* the light, not against it. Dark figures that stride along the refracted beams like travelers on glass roads, pausing when you pause, retreating when you step forward. They have no features — only the suggestion of posture, the implication of a head turning to regard you with whatever passes for sight in a creature made of subtracted illumination. When the lens stops its rotation for a beat, they freeze too, perfectly still, perfectly patient.

You tear your eyes away. The narrow iron walkway circles the lens at chest height, its railing cold and pitted. Ahead — the only direction that feels like progress — a brass door is set into the curving wall, leading up to the lantern room. But the door will not simply open.

Three rotating discs are mounted in a recessed lock-plate, each engraved with symbols: spirals, hourglasses, eyes within eyes. They turn freely, clicking through positions with mechanical precision. You've seen these symbols before. The cipher-wheel in your possession bears their twins — a key waiting for its lock, or a lock waiting for its key.

The shadows lean closer through the light. The lens begins another rotation.

You have the cipher-wheel. You have the symbols. Align them correctly, and the door will open with a grinding click that echoes like a jaw unclenching.

Misalign them, and the light will find you.

It is already looking.
