The chamber opens like the ribcage of a leviathan that swallowed the wrong century.

Gears rise around you — not arranged but *assembled*, a vertical cathedral of iron and brass that stretches upward into shadow. Cogs the size of waterwheels interlock with smaller mechanisms in frozen constellations, each tooth meeting its neighbor in a pattern that suggests not engineering but appetite. Chains hang slack between sprockets like the ligaments of some enormous skeleton. The whole apparatus has the stillness of a cathedral organ mid-chord, as though the machine exhaled once and forgot to breathe back in.

You are standing inside a clock. Or perhaps a clock is standing inside you. The scale is wrong either way — intimate and infinite, like finding a city inside your own heartbeat.

Everything is still. That is the wrongness. This was meant to *move*. You can feel it in the teeth of every gear, in the taut slack of every chain — a machine designed for perpetual motion, frozen mid-breath. Dust has settled into the grooves like sediment in dead riverbeds.

One gear, larger than the rest, dominates the chamber's heart. It is seized — not rusted but *drowned*. Black grease pools in its teeth like tar in a rotted molar, weeping from its hub in slow, dark tears, thick and deliberate, as though someone poured grief into the machine's mouth to keep it from speaking.

A brass plate catches your torchlight, bolted to the wall with screws the size of your thumb:

**AT ZERO — THE LIGHT DIES.**

Below it, a countdown ticks with the quiet desperation of a man knocking on a door no one will answer. The digits are mechanical — tiny flaps of tin that drop to reveal each new number, and the sound they make is like teeth falling from a mouth. **00:03:47.** Then **00:03:46.**

You trace the mechanism's logic upward. Every gear connects to the next, a chain of frozen intention leading toward the central axle — a column of iron thick as a lighthouse within the lighthouse itself. At its heart: a socket, hexagonal, yawning. Waiting. Sized for a crank-handle, the kind you'd use to wind a mechanism back from the dead.

The grease is old but not impenetrable. Oil could loosen what time has welded shut — free the gear, accept a crank. And if the crank turned — you can almost hear it: a groan of iron waking, chains snapping taut, the great gears grinding into motion like the bones of a titan remembering how to walk. The numbers would stop their terrible descent. The light would hold.

But that future is not yet written. The numbers keep falling. Whatever happens at zero, the brass plate promises nothing good.

The clock ticks. The grease glistens. The darkness leans closer, listening.
