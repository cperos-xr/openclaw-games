The spiral staircase delivers you into a room that breathes.

Not with air — with absence. The cot sags beneath a threadbare blanket folded with impossible neatness, the kind of care that speaks of routine calcified into ritual. Someone made this bed every morning. Someone straightened the corners long after it mattered. The pillow holds a faint depression, a ghost-weight, as though the sleeper merely stepped out and will return before the dust settles.

He won't.

The bookshelves groan under tide tables brittle as dead leaves. Maritime charts yellow on the walls, their coastlines hand-inked in a trembling hand — steadier in the early pages, then devolving into something frantic, the shore rendered over and over as if the drawer feared forgetting its shape. A pencil snaps in two on the desk. Both pieces point the same direction: toward the window, toward the sea.

And above the cot, the portrait.

Silas Thorne regards you with the patient hunger of a man who has been waiting. The oil has cracked along his cheekbones like fault lines in dry earth, but his eyes — his eyes are untouched by time. They are wet. They follow the exact center of your chest, not your face, as though watching something behind your ribs. You step left. The gaze tracks. You step right. It tracks. The frame creaks softly, though there is no draft.

At the foot of the bed sits a heavy trunk, iron-bound and rust-eaten. The lock is a mouth frozen mid-sentence. You tug at the lid. It does not yield. The keyhole gapes, expectant — shaped for something old, something specific.

You press the pillow. Beneath it, a single page torn from a journal, the handwriting barely legible, pressed so hard the pen nearly scored through:

*The mechanism must not reach zero.*

The words smell of salt and lamp oil. The ink is still dark. Far too dark for fifty years. Far too dark for five.

Behind you, the portrait's painted hand seems closer to the edge of its frame than it was before.

You decide not to confirm this.
