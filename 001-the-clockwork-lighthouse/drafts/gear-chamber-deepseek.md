# Gear Chamber — Draft by Desmond Vex (writer-deepseek)

The staircase opened into vastness and you stopped breathing.

The chamber was enormous — fifty feet across, the full diameter of the lighthouse, and it went up and down further than your light could reach. And it was *full*. Every cubic foot of space was consumed by machinery: gears the size of wagon wheels, cogs interlocked in chains that stretched from floor to ceiling, shafts and axles and drive belts forming a mechanical cathedral that hummed with a sound so deep it was almost silence. The vibration in the walls below had its source here. This was the heart of the lighthouse, and it was enormous, and it was *wrong*.

Most of the gears were frozen. Locked in place by rust and time, they formed a static sculpture of impossible complexity. But the mechanism wasn't dead — somewhere in the tangle, something still turned. You could hear it: a slow, laborious rotation, each revolution accompanied by a grinding protest of metal against metal. The countdown was still running. It had been running for fifty years, winding down.

You found the central axle — a shaft as thick as your body, rising vertically through the chamber's center. At its base, a socket was cut into the bronze housing: hexagonal, polished by repeated use, exactly the size and shape of the crank-handle in your pocket. And next to it, etched into a brass plate bolted to the housing, two words:

**AT ZERO — THE LIGHT DIES.**

Around the main gear — the largest, twelve feet in diameter — numbers were etched in descending order. A countdown. You traced them with your finger and found the current position:

**00:03:47.**

Three minutes and forty-seven seconds. The mechanism was counting down to something, and it was almost there.

Between the socket and the main gear, one massive cog was seized — the teeth locked against its neighbor, black with old grease and corrosion. That was why the mechanism was dying. That was why the light stuttered. The gear couldn't turn, and without it turning, the countdown could only slow, never reset.

You pulled the oil can from your pack. The seized gear was at chest height, accessible from a narrow catwalk that bridged two frozen shafts. You climbed out over the abyss — the chamber dropped into darkness below you — and poured oil into the gear's teeth. The old grease resisted, then softened, then ran in dark rivulets down the mechanism's face.

You waited. Then you braced yourself and pushed the gear. It resisted — your arms trembled — and then, with a sound like a cracking bone, it *moved*. One tooth. Then two. Then it was turning on its own, grinding into its neighbor with increasing ease.

The whole chamber shuddered. Gears above you began to turn. The deep hum shifted in pitch, rising from subsonic moan to something almost musical. The mechanism was waking up.

You climbed back to the central axle and fitted the crank-handle into the socket. It seated with a click that resonated through the entire lighthouse. You turned it — once, twice, three times — and the mechanism roared to life. Chains tightened. Shafts spun. The great gear began to rotate with terrible, beautiful purpose.

The numbers on the countdown ticked: **00:05:12**. Then **00:05:13**. It was climbing. Resetting.

You kept cranking until your arms gave out. The chamber sang around you, every gear turning in perfect concert, and for a moment you understood what this place was supposed to be: not a lighthouse. A clock. A clock that measured something vast and terrible, and held it back.

The countdown stabilized at **00:59:59**. One hour. The mechanism would keep the light burning for one hour.

You had work to do.
