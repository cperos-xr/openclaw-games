The chamber opens like the ribcage of a leviathan that swallowed the wrong century.

Gears the size of waterwheels climb the curved walls in frozen constellations — not arranged, you realize, but *grown*. They cluster like barnacles on a whale's jaw, each tooth interlocking with its neighbor in a pattern that suggests not engineering but appetite. Chains hang slack as hanged men between sprockets. The whole apparatus has the stillness of a cathedral organ mid-chord, as though the machine exhaled once and forgot to breathe back in.

You are standing inside a clock. Or perhaps a clock is standing inside you. The scale is wrong either way — intimate and infinite, like finding a city inside your own heartbeat.

A brass plate catches your torchlight, bolted to the wall with screws the size of your thumb:

**AT ZERO — THE LIGHT DIES**

Below it, a countdown ticks with the quiet desperation of a man knocking on a door no one will answer. **00:03:47.** The digits are mechanical — tiny flaps of tin that drop to reveal each new number, and the sound they make is like teeth falling from a mouth.

You trace the mechanism's logic upward. Every gear connects to the next, a chain of frozen intention leading toward the central axle — a column of iron thick as a lighthouse within the lighthouse itself. At its heart: a socket, hexagonal, yawning. Waiting.

But one gear has seized. Not rusted — *drowned*. Black grease pools in its teeth like tar in a rotted molar, thick and deliberate, as though someone poured grief into the machine's mouth to keep it from speaking. The grease is still wet.

The countdown drops to **00:03:41**.

The socket in the axle is the right size for a crank. The seized gear is the wrong color for anything living. And somewhere above you, the lighthouse beam falters, stutters, and nearly — nearly —

The clock ticks. The grease glistens. The darkness leans closer, listening.
