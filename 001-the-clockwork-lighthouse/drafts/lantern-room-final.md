# Lantern Room

The stairs end. The world opens. And the sky is wrong.

Above you, where stars should be, there is nothing — a void-black dome that presses down like a hand on the back of your neck. No moon. No clouds. Just darkness so absolute it feels solid, as though the sea swallowed the heavens and forgot to give them back.

The lantern room is the lighthouse's crown, open to the wind on three sides, the fourth walled with thick glass panels warped by age into lenses that bend the ocean into rippling vertical curtains. Salt spray freezes on your skin the moment it lands. The wind screams through the gaps with a sound like something trying to get in, or something trying to get out.

And in the center — the lamp.

Not fire. Not electricity. Something older and stranger: a mechanical heart of crystal and brass that burns with a cold, blue-white radiance that casts no warmth. The beam emerges from its core and cuts across the churning sea — a blade of wrong light that stutters and pulses with the dying mechanism below. Each flicker sends shadows lurching across the glass walls in directions that have nothing to do with the light source. The shadows move like they're breathing.

Through the glass floor panels — thick, warped, like standing on frozen water — you can see it all. The entire mechanism. Every gear, every chain, every frozen axle, laid out below you like an anatomical diagram of a clockwork heart. And the water. The sea has reached the cliff base. The dock is submerged. Waves climb the rock face with a patience that feels personal.

The countdown reads **00:00:12**.

Twelve seconds.

A brass pedestal rises directly beneath the lamp. Simple. A column of bronze with a single keyhole shaped like a clock hand. Your fingers find the clockwork-key in your pocket. It's warm. It's been waiting.

The air between you and the pedestal shimmers — not with heat, but with *presence*. Something has been standing here the whole time, waiting for you to see it.

He materializes the way a word appears when you finally remember it. Gaunt. Hollow-cheeked. Eyes like the polished stones in his portrait, fifty years downstairs. Silas Thorne. The last keeper. Dead for half a century, but he is here the way a promise is here — not alive, not gone, but *held*.

The countdown reads **00:00:07**.

His mouth moves. The wind carries his words like they've been stored in the mechanism for decades: *"Turn the key. Reset the light. Before the sea takes it all."*

He steps aside. Not with fear — with relief. The look of a man who has been holding a door for fifty years and finally feels someone else grab the handle.

**00:00:04.**

You step forward. The key slides into the clockwork keyhole with a click that resonates through every shaft and gear and chain in the lighthouse, through the stone bones and iron veins, through the entire impossible machine. You turn it.

The mechanism *reverses*.

Below you, gears stutter — then spin backward — then catch, then forward, then *find their rhythm*. The grinding dies. The stuttering dies. The hum shifts from the death-rattle of winding-down to the deep, steady pulse of something *alive*. The countdown ticks up: 00:01:00. 00:05:00. 00:10:00. The numbers climb like a man pulling himself out of deep water. Reset. Stabilized. *Running.*

The lamp changes. The blue-white wrongness bleeds away, and in its place — warmth. Golden light, the color of sunset seen through honey, pours from the crystal core. The beam sweeps across the sea, steady, reliable, *right*. No stutter. No flicker. A lighthouse doing what a lighthouse was always meant to do.

Through the glass floor, you watch the water retreat. The island rises. The cliffs emerge, dripping, and the sea falls back to its proper mark. The dock reappears — battered, groaning, but above water. Your motorboat still bobs at its post.

Silas stands at the glass wall, watching the golden beam cut across calm waves. The wind touches him and he doesn't ripple. He turns to you. The urgency is gone from his face. What remains is the portrait's expression — but at peace. A man who has finally finished his watch.

He raises one hand — the gesture from the lens room, the figure in the refractions — and then he is light. Golden light, the same color as the beam, folding into the lamp's core and becoming part of its steady, warm, unending pulse.

The lighthouse keeper has gone home.

The beam sweeps the sea. The island holds. The gears turn, counting not time remaining, but time preserved.

You breathe.
