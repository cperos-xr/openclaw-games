You climb the last iron rung and the sky opens.

The lantern room is a cage of wind and salt-spray. Three sides are nothing — just the dark Atlantic heaving under stars, the horizon line bent like a bruise. The fourth wall is glass, old glass, thick with bubbles and imperfections that split the light into frozen tears. And the light — God, the light. It burns from a lamp the size of a casket, blue-white as a surgeon's table, wrong in a way that makes your fillings ache. The beam sweeps seaward with a sound like a held breath.

Beneath your feet, the floor is glass too. You wish it weren't. Below, the mechanism churns — a cathedral of brass gears grinding in patterns too large to comprehend, teeth meshing with the slow inevitability of tectonic plates. Through it, you see dark water climbing. The island is sinking. Twelve seconds.

*Tick. Tick. Tick.*

The air beside the brass pedestal shivers. Silas materializes like ink dropped in water — not the raging thing you've seen in paintings, but a man exhausted past fear. His outline flickers with the lamp's terrible rhythm. When he speaks, his voice is the sound of a clock spring unwinding.

"Turn the key. Reset the light. Before the sea takes it all."

Your hands are already moving. The clockwork key slides into the pedestal's keyhole — that clock-hand shape you've traced a hundred times in your mind — and it fits the way a word fits a mouth, the way a wave fits a shore.

You turn it.

And everything *sighs*.

The mechanism below reverses. You watch the great gears catch, stutter, then begin to grind the other direction — not with violence, but with the gentle certainty of a music box remembering its song. The water drops. The island exhales. And the light shifts from that sterile blue-white to something warm. Something gold. Candlelight in a window. The amber of old honey. The color of a lighthouse that remembers what it's *for*.

The beam still sweeps, but now it calls ships home.

Silas is fading. Not fading — *settling*. His edges soften into the warm light like frost meeting dawn. He doesn't speak again. He doesn't need to. His face holds something past gratitude, past relief — the expression of a man who finally hears the clock tick correctly after a century of wrongness.

He goes gentle. The light stays.

The island holds.
