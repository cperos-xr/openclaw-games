# Supply Room

The door groans open on seized hinges, and you squeeze into a space that was never meant for comfort. You have to turn sideways to fit between the shelving units — they press in from both sides like the ribs of some long-dead thing. The ceiling is low enough to touch. Every surface is cluttered: coils of rope gone stiff and brown, tins with their labels dissolved to grey pulp, wrenches and spanners heaped in corroded piles. Someone organized this once. You can see the ghost of a system — hooks labeled with faded ink, shelves divided by type of tool. But that was before whoever worked here stopped caring.

Your boots splash. The floor is wet. Not puddles — something worse. A slow, steady seep of seawater pushing up through hairline cracks in the concrete, darkening the stone in spreading stains. You can taste it in the air, that mineral brine that's been haunting every room in this lighthouse. The island is sinking. Has been for years, probably. This room is below the waterline now, and the sea wants it back.

The workbench takes up most of the back wall. Brass components litter its surface — gears, springs, tiny threaded bolts — the guts of some mechanism pulled apart and never reassembled. You push a few pieces aside and freeze. Carved deep into the wood, scored so heavily that the grain splintered around it: a circle with a vertical line bisecting a smaller triangle, nested inside a spiral. Not quite a letter, not quite a diagram. The edges have gone soft with swelling grain, but whoever carved this meant it. Meant it the way a warning is meant. You trace it with your fingertip and feel the gouges catch on your skin.

On the bench's far edge, half-buried under a canvas rag, sits a heavy tin of machine oil. The label is gone, but when you shake it, something thick and viscous sloshes inside. Still usable. Beside the door — on a small iron hook you almost missed — hangs a key. Rust has eaten into its surface like a disease, practically flaking onto your fingers when you lift it. But the teeth are intact. People don't hang keys by doors unless the doors matter.

You take both. The floor seeps on.

## Look Descriptions

[LOOK: shelves] Wooden shelving units sag under the weight of rusted tools — pliers with fused jaws, screwdrivers worn to nubs, a handsaw whose teeth have gone the color of old blood. Faded ink labels still cling to the hooks, ghosts of a system that collapsed long ago.

[LOOK: workbench] A broad workbench dominates the back wall, its surface buried under disassembled brass components — gears, springs, tiny threaded bolts. The wood is scarred and water-warped. A strange symbol has been gouged deep into its center.

[LOOK: floor-cracks] Hairline cracks web across the concrete floor, each one dark with moisture. Seawater wells up through them in a slow, steady seep, spreading damp stains across the stone. The floor squelches underfoot. The island is below the waterline here.

[LOOK: cans] Tin cans crowd the lower shelves, their labels long dissolved into illegible brown smears. Some have rusted shut; others have split open, revealing nothing but dried residue inside.

[LOOK: rope] Coils of rope sit in stiff, salt-crusted loops on the upper shelf. The fibers have gone brittle and brown. Whatever load it once held, the rope hasn't been touched in years.

[LOOK: symbol] A circle bisects a smaller triangle, nested inside a spiral — scratched so deep into the workbench that splintered wood fans out around the gouges. It doesn't match any engineering diagram you know. The edges are worn soft, but the intent behind the carving is unmistakable. Someone needed this mark to last.
