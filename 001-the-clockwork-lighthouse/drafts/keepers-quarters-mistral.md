# Keeper's Quarters — Draft by The Muse (writer-mistral-creative)

The spiral staircase delivered you into a room that shouldn't feel lived-in but somehow did. Not clean — not maintained — but *inhabited*. The cot's blanket was threadbare but folded, the way someone folds a blanket when they expect to come back. Books on the shelves stood upright, spines aligned. The air carried a faint warmth that had no source.

You caught yourself holding your breath.

The portrait dominated the far wall. Oil on canvas, darkened by decades of candle smoke, but the face emerged from the gloom with unsettling clarity: a gaunt man with hollow cheeks and eyes like polished stones. Silas Thorne, the last keeper, according to the brass plate beneath. His expression wasn't grim or sorrowful — it was *urgent*. The look of a man trying to tell you something through glass.

You moved left. His eyes followed. You moved right. They followed again. You told yourself it was the light. The candle you didn't have. The lamp that wasn't lit.

The bookshelves held maritime charts annotated in a cramped hand — tide tables for dates that hadn't happened yet, lunar cycles extending decades into the future. Someone had planned to be here a long time. Or someone had been calculating how much time was left.

The trunk sat at the foot of the cot like a patient animal. Iron-banded, old, locked. The keyhole matched the small iron key you'd found in the supply room — you were sure of it. Inside, wrapped in oilcloth that crumbled at your touch: a brass crank-handle, heavy and warm, and a cipher-wheel — a disc of etched brass with concentric rings of symbols that shifted as you turned them.

Under the pillow, a single page torn from a journal. The handwriting was Silas's — you recognized it from the desk downstairs. Just one line, written three times, each iteration smaller and more desperate:

*"The mechanism must not reach zero."*

You looked at the portrait. Silas looked back.

Something cold touched your ankle. You looked down. Nothing there. But the floor was wet.
