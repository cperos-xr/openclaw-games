The door groans open on seized hinges, and you squeeze into a space that was never meant for comfort. The supply room is barely wider than your arm-span, choked floor to ceiling with the remnants of practical necessity. Shelves sag under the weight of rusted tools — pliers with fused jaws, screwdrivers worn to nubs, a handsaw whose teeth have gone the color of old blood. Coiled rope sits in stiff loops, crusted with salt. Tin cans crowd the back shelf, their labels long dissolved into illegible brown smears.

Your boots splash. The floor is wet.

Not puddles — a slow, persistent seep, welling up through hairline cracks in the stone. Seawater. You can taste it in the air, that mineral brine that's been haunting every room in this lighthouse. The island is sinking. Has been for years, probably. The cracks are new enough to be alarming, old enough that no one bothered to fix them. No one was left to fix them.

Against the west wall, a workbench. Brass components litter its surface — gears, springs, tiny threaded bolts — the guts of some mechanism pulled apart and never reassembled. You push a few pieces aside and freeze. Carved into the wood, deep and deliberate, is a symbol you don't recognize. Not quite a letter, not quite a diagram. It looks like an eye inside a spiral, scored so heavily that the grain splintered around it. Whoever carved this meant it. Meant it the way a warning is meant.

You scan the room again, slower this time. On a low shelf, half-buried under canvas, you find an oil can — heavy tin, dented but intact. A shake produces that satisfying glug of something still liquid inside. Useful. Beside the door, on a small iron hook, hangs a key. It's small, rough with oxidation, practically flaking rust onto your fingers when you lift it. A key to what, you don't know yet. But people don't hang keys by doors unless the doors matter.

You take both. The floor seeps on.
