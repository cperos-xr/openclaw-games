The Lens Room is a cathedral of glass.

The great Fresnel lens rises before you like a crystal beehive — concentric rings of prismatic glass mounted in a brass armature, fifteen feet tall, each ring cut with mathematical precision to bend light into a single focused beam. It should be beautiful. It *is* beautiful. But it isn't working right.

The lens rotates, but jerkily — stuttering through its revolution in fits and starts, casting fractured light across every surface in patterns that form shapes if you look too long. Faces. Hands reaching. Mouths open in silent instruction. Geometries that almost resolve into something human, then dissolve as the lens grinds another quarter-turn. You try not to look at them. They don't seem to notice you. That is worse.

The shadows are worse still.

They move *in* the light, not against it. Dark figures that stride along the refracted beams like travelers on glass roads — silhouettes that form and dissolve with each stutter, pausing when you pause, retreating when you step forward. They have no features, only the suggestion of posture, the implication of a head turning to regard you with whatever passes for sight in a creature made of subtracted illumination. Where the fractured beams touch your skin, you feel something: not pain, not cold, but *recognition*. As if the light knows you. When the lens stops its rotation for a beat, they freeze too — perfectly still, perfectly patient.

You tear your eyes away. The narrow iron walkway circles the lens at chest height, its railing cold and pitted, green with verdigris in places. Ahead — the only direction that feels like progress — a brass door is set into the curving wall, heavier than the others, with a mechanism embedded in its surface. Leading up to the lantern room. But the door will not simply open.

Three rotating discs are mounted in a recessed lock-plate, each engraved with symbols: spirals, hourglasses, eyes within eyes. They turn freely, clicking through positions with mechanical precision. You've seen these symbols before. The cipher-wheel in your pocket bears their twins — a key waiting for its lock, or a lock waiting for its key.

The shadows lean closer through the light. The lens begins another rotation.

You have the cipher-wheel. You have the symbols. Align them correctly, and the door will open with a grinding click that echoes like a jaw unclenching — not a lock releasing, but a gear engaging, as if the door itself were part of the lighthouse's machinery.

Misalign them, and the light will find you.

It is already looking.
